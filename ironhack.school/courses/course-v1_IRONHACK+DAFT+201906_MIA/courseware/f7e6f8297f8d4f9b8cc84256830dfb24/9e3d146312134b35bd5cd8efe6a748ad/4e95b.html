Categorical variables allow you to group records in different ways, and each way that you categorize them can provide you with a different perspective when you're conducting your analysis. A common way of creating additional categorical fields is to bin numeric variables in a column based on how relatively high or low they are.</p> <p>For example, our data set has a Combined MPG variable that tells us how many miles per gallon of gasoline the vehicle can travel - in other words, the vehicle's fuel efficiency. However, it isn't easy to tell whether a vehicle has low or high fuel efficiency just by looking at the numbers. To help with this, we can bin the values in the Combined MPG field into an intuitive number of categories.</p> <p>For this column, let's choose 5 bins so that we have one middle/moderate bin, two relatively low and high bins outside of that, and then two very low/high bins at the ends.</p> <p> <a href="https://camo.githubusercontent.com/8807ecba60dc7abb01c75efbb4f520e2cc1257d4/68747470733a2f2f73332d65752d776573742d312e616d617a6f6e6177732e636f6d2f69682d6d6174657269616c732f75706c6f6164732f646174612d7374617469632f696d616765732f666976652d62696e732e706e67" target="_blank" rel="nofollow"> <img src="https://camo.githubusercontent.com/8807ecba60dc7abb01c75efbb4f520e2cc1257d4/68747470733a2f2f73332d65752d776573742d312e616d617a6f6e6177732e636f6d2f69682d6d6174657269616c732f75706c6f6164732f646174612d7374617469632f696d616765732f666976652d62696e732e706e67" alt="Five Bins" data-canonical-src="https://s3-eu-west-1.amazonaws.com/ih-materials/uploads/data-static/images/five-bins.png" style="max-width:100%;"> </a> </p> <p>In Python, let's create a list of labels that will be assigned to our bins.</p> <pre> <code>mpg_labels = ['Very Low', 'Low', 'Moderate', 'High', 'Very High'] </code> </pre> <p>Next, we must determine how we want our data to be binned. There are three main approaches that we can choose from:</p> <ul> <li> <strong>Equal width bins:</strong> the range for each bin is the same size. </li> <li> <strong>Equal frequency bins:</strong> approximately the same number of records in each bin. </li> <li> <strong>Custom-sized bins:</strong> the user explicitly defines where they want the cutoff for each bin to be. </li> </ul> <p>If you want equal width bins, you can use the <code>cut</code> method in Pandas and pass it the column you want to bin, the number of bins and the list of labels. </p> <pre> <code>bins = pd.cut(data['Combined MPG'],5, labels=mpg_labels) bins.head(10) 0 Low 1 Very Low 2 Very Low 3 Very Low 4 Very Low 5 Low 6 Low 7 Low 8 Low 9 Low </code> </pre> <p>If you want equal frequency bins, you would use the <code>qcut</code> method instead with all the same inputs. </p> <pre> <code>bins = pd.qcut(data['Combined MPG'],5, labels=mpg_labels) bins.head(10) 0 Low 1 Very Low 2 Very Low 3 Very Low 4 Very Low 5 High 6 High 7 Moderate 8 High 9 High </code> </pre> <p>Note the difference in results. With equal width binning, there will be some bins that contain more records than others (such as the Low bin). With equal frequency binning, some of those records will be forced into other bins (e.g. the Moderate bin and even the High bin). This is an important consideration when determining how you want to categorize your data.</p> <p>Finally, if you want custom bin sizes, you can pass a list of bin range values to the <code>cut</code> method instead of the number of bins, and it will bin the values for you accordingly. </p> <pre> <code>cutoffs = [7,14,21,23,30,40] bins = pd.cut(data['Combined MPG'],cutoffs, labels=mpg_labels) bins.head(10) 0 Low 1 Very Low 2 Low 3 Very Low 4 Low 5 Moderate 6 High 7 Low 8 Moderate 9 High </code> </pre> <h2> <a id="user-content-conditional-categories" class="anchor" href="#conditional-categories" aria-hidden="true"> <span aria-hidden="true" class="octicon octicon-link"></span> </a>Conditional Categories </h2> <p>Another way to create intuitive additional categories in your data is to create columns based on conditional statements. Earlier in this lesson, we filtered our data based on conditional statements. Here, we will populate the values in a column based on them using the <code>loc</code> method. </p> <p>Our vehicles data set currently has 45 different values in the Transmission field, but one of the key pieces of information embedded in there is whether a vehicle has an automatic or manual transmission. Let's look at how we can create a new TransType column that only contains one of two values for each vehicle: Automatic or Manual.</p> <pre> <code>data.loc[data['Transmission'].str.startswith('A'), 'TransType'] = 'Automatic' data.loc[data['Transmission'].str.startswith('M'), 'TransType'] = 'Manual' </code> </pre> <p>We were able to leverage the <code>str.startswith</code> method in our conditional statements such that whenever the value in the Transmission field started with an A, we would assign a TransType of Automatic, and when the value started with M, we would assign a TransType of Manual. </p> <h2> <a id="user-content-one-hot-encoding-categorical-variables" class="anchor" href="#one-hot-encoding-categorical-variables" aria-hidden="true"> <span aria-hidden="true" class="octicon octicon-link"></span> </a>One-Hot Encoding Categorical Variables </h2> <p>One-hot encoding is a technique used to expand a single categorical column into as many columns as there are categories. Pandas provides us with a few different ways to do this. The first way is by merging.
